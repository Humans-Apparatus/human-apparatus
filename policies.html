Privacy Policy

Effective from first public release · Revised as needed

Human Apparatus is a non-profit software foundation. We build tools for humans. This means our relationship with your data is simple: we want as little of it as possible.

What we do not collect

  • Usage telemetry of any kind
  • Crash reports transmitted without explicit opt-in
  • Behavioral analytics or session tracking
  • Device fingerprints or hardware identifiers
  • Location data
  • Any data sold, licensed, or shared with advertisers

What this website collects

This website is a static HTML file. It has no backend, no cookies, no tracking scripts, and no analytics. If hosted on GitHub Pages, GitHub's standard server logs may record IP addresses per their own privacy policy. We do not access or process those logs.

What our software collects

Each tool in the Human Apparatus suite operates locally by default. No data leaves your machine unless a feature explicitly requires network access, and those features are always clearly labelled, opt-in, and auditable in the source code.

All software is open source. If you want to verify what any tool sends over the network, the code is available and readable.

Third-party services

Downloads are served through GitHub Releases. GitHub has its own privacy policy. We do not operate any proprietary download infrastructure.

Contact

Questions about privacy can be raised as a public issue on our GitHub organisation, or sent by email once we publish a contact address post-registration.

Last updated: 2025. This document is version-controlled at github.com/human-apparatus.

Academic Policy

For researchers, educators, and students

Human Apparatus tools are free software and may be used freely in academic research, teaching, and publication without restriction beyond the terms of their respective open source licenses.

Research use

You may use, modify, and study any Human Apparatus tool for research purposes. You may publish findings, benchmarks, critiques, and comparisons without prior approval. We welcome scrutiny. If your research identifies a flaw, please disclose it responsibly and we will address it publicly.

Classroom and educational use

Human Apparatus tools may be freely used in university courses, workshops, bootcamps, and self-directed study. Educators may distribute modified versions under the terms of the applicable license. No licensing fee, institutional agreement, or permission is required.

Citation guidance

If you publish work that uses a Human Apparatus tool, we ask (but do not require) that you cite the specific tool and version used. Repository URLs and version tags are available on GitHub. A plain-text citation is sufficient — no formal academic format is mandated.

Forking for academic purposes

Forking any Human Apparatus repository for academic study or experimentation is explicitly encouraged. You do not need to notify us, though we appreciate hearing about interesting uses.

What we ask in return

  • Accurate representation of what our tools do and don't do
  • Responsible disclosure of security or privacy issues before publication
  • Attribution where attribution is due

We do not ask for co-authorship, licensing fees, or approval rights over academic work. Your research is yours.

Last updated: 2025. Questions: open an issue at github.com/human-apparatus.
Oath

Oath Against Zawinski's Observation

On the rejection of feature accumulation and the virtue of modularity

We reject the tendency of software to endlessly accumulate features until clarity, identity, and human operability are lost beneath complexity.

At Human Apparatus, every sufficiently distinct capability deserves its own tool, its own interface, and its own purpose.

We believe software should remain

  • Comprehensible to its operators at every layer
  • Modular — composed of bounded, named things
  • Inspectable — readable by a human without special tooling
  • Text-native — where the substrate is plain language, not binary state
  • Human-scaled — small enough to be understood, large enough to be useful

Complexity must be federated, not absorbed. When a system grows too large to hold in one mind, the answer is decomposition — not documentation, not abstraction layers, not a larger mind.

«Every sufficiently distinct feature shall result in a new product rather than another version of the old.»
Ecosystems over monoliths. Composition over accumulation. Apparatus over platform sprawl.

Human Apparatus exists to build software humans can still fully understand.

Adopted at founding. All contributors to Human Apparatus projects are asked to hold this oath in mind when making design decisions.
Oath

Oath of Borderless Tools

On the geographic and economic neutrality of software

A tool that works only in certain places, for certain people, under certain economic conditions is not a tool — it is a service contingent on the goodwill of its provider.

We build tools. They belong to no jurisdiction. They serve all humans.

We hold

  • That useful software should function without an internet connection
  • That no feature should be withheld based on geography or nationality
  • That no license should impose conditions based on the user's country of residence
  • That pricing models which exclude the global majority are not neutral — they are political
  • That free software is an infrastructure question, not a generosity question
  • That a terminal and a text file are universal substrates, accessible across the full range of computing hardware that humans actually use

What this means in practice

Human Apparatus tools are local-first by design. A user in Lagos, Karachi, or Chișinău with an eight-year-old laptop and an intermittent connection should have full access to every feature any other user has.

We will not build features that require cloud accounts, subscription keys, or network connectivity to function. Where network features exist, they are additive and optional.

We will not impose export restrictions beyond those required by applicable law, and we will be transparent about any such legal constraint as they arise.

On language

We recognise that English-only tooling excludes the majority of the world. We commit to internationalisation infrastructure in every tool we build, and we welcome contributions that extend our tools into other languages and scripts.

«A tool that cannot be used by all of humanity is not yet finished.»
Local-first over cloud-dependent. Open over gated. Universal over optimised for the privileged.
Adopted at founding. This oath informs all architecture and distribution decisions.
Standard

Community Standards

How we treat each other

Human Apparatus is a small project with a clear purpose. We do not need a lengthy code of conduct — we need a clear one.

The standard

Treat every contributor, user, and critic as an intelligent adult. Assume good faith. Disagree with ideas, not people. If you are wrong, say so. If someone else is wrong, say that too — plainly, without hostility.

What is expected

  • Accurate, honest communication about what code does and doesn't do
  • Patience with contributors at different experience levels
  • Keeping discussion focused on the work
  • Accepting that maintainers make final decisions, without drama
  • Responsible disclosure of security issues before public discussion

What is not acceptable

  • Harassment, personal attacks, or sustained hostility toward any person
  • Deliberate exclusion on the basis of identity, nationality, or background
  • Misrepresenting the project, its maintainers, or its contributors
  • Introducing deliberate defects, backdoors, or malicious code

Enforcement

Violations are handled by project maintainers. First instance: a private message. Continued behaviour: removal from project spaces. There is no bureaucratic appeals process — this is a small project, not a corporation.

Reports can be made by opening a private issue or emailing the maintainer directly once a contact address is published.

Licensing Policy

Why we are free software, and which licenses we use

Human Apparatus publishes all software under free and open source licenses. This is not a growth strategy. It is a commitment to the users and communities we exist to serve.

Our position on licensing

We distinguish between open source (a development methodology) and free software (a political and ethical position). Human Apparatus holds both positions simultaneously. Our software is free in the sense of liberty, and developed openly in the sense of process.

We do not use open-core models, source-available licenses, or business-source licenses. There is no paid tier with locked features. The software you can download is the complete software.

Licenses used

  • MIT — default for libraries and utilities where maximum reuse is desirable
  • GPL v3 — for tools where we want derivative works to remain free
  • AGPL v3 — for any networked tool where closing the loop on SaaS derivatives matters

Each repository states its license clearly in the root LICENSE file and in package metadata. If you are unsure which license applies to a specific project, the repository is the authoritative source.

Contributor licensing

Contributors retain copyright over their contributions. By submitting a pull request, you agree to license your contribution under the same license as the project you are contributing to. We do not require a CLA (Contributor License Agreement).

Use in proprietary software

MIT-licensed Human Apparatus tools may be used in proprietary software. GPL and AGPL-licensed tools may not be incorporated into proprietary software without triggering the copyleft requirement. This is intentional. The license choice per project reflects how strongly we want to protect that tool's freedom downstream.
